Questions Answered

The clinic visit exists for the patient's questions, but runs on the physician's.

Published March 1, 2026. Verified public link: Substack.

Not every patient can be saved, but his illness may be eased by the way the doctor responds to him. - Anatole Broyard

There is a dichotomy of illness.

For the patient, illness is a lived experience. A pain in the lower back that will not go away, or episodes of waking up on the ground, surrounded by strangers. The fear that this is something bad. The anxiety of not knowing how it will turn out. For the physician, illness is an intellectual practice - a puzzle to be solved, a diagnosis to be made, medication prescribed, a follow-up scheduled.

Underneath this lies a second dichotomy: power. The physician, armed with knowledge and training, tells the patient what they are suffering, how they will suffer, and how to ease that suffering. The patient, vulnerable and without that knowledge, receives this - accepts it like accepting a verdict.

And then there is a third: who holds the questions, and who is left answering them.

The visit exists for the patient's questions, but it runs on the physician's.

Some have clean answers: "When did it start?" "Are you left-handed or right-handed?" Others do not. "What does your seizure look like?"

Here the patient is forced to narrate a lived experience from an angle they have never considered. The experience existed all along - they just never had to put it into words.

"They said it is a seizure." Or: "It looks like a seizure."

I need more than that. "What do you mean by seizure?" I ask. "What does it look like?" I follow up. Sometimes the patient calls a family member into the conversation - someone who witnessed what they cannot remember.

We guide patients through this. After ten minutes of questions, a visit that started as "I'm here for seizures" turns into:

My seizure starts with a strange taste, then I hear a melody, then my right hand twitches, then I black out. I bite the left side of my tongue. I started having this weird taste ten years ago, but never thought much of it. Sometimes I wake up with blood on my pillow, sometimes my muscles are sore. No family history of epilepsy. No seizures as a child, no head trauma, my birth was uneventful, I grew up fine.

This is necessary work. It is the physician's job to elicit the pertinent history. Pertinent - a word drilled into us through training. We learn to tell signal from noise, to shape a raw story into something that shines with diagnostic clarity. With experience, we get good at this.

But the patient is doing something harder. They are narrating for the first time. It is like telling any story - the first time through, it comes out scattered, uncertain. After even one run of telling it, the narrative flows. A patient seeing a doctor for a new concern has a million worries around "blacking out" and no structure to hold them. Physician and patient construct the narrative together.

But the construction is guided by the physician. It serves the physician more than the patient.

And patients have their own questions. The "one more thing." The "Doctor, I have another question." These get pushed to the margins as the clock runs out and the next patient knocks on the door. Physicians construct a narrative that makes sense to them by asking questions. Patients need answers to their questions to construct their own version - of the diagnosis, of what it means, of what comes next. But the visit ends with patients having answered many questions and asked few, if any. The answers they do receive are brief, rehearsed.

But what if the third dichotomy did not have to be one?

Not a new room. Not a longer visit. Just a different sequence. What if, before the patient walks in, they have already had the chance to tell their story - on their own terms, at their own pace?

Imagine the night before an appointment. The patient opens something on their phone - a conversation, not a form. It does not diagnose. It does not explain. It asks. The way a good physician would, but without the clock: "What is the very first thing you notice before a seizure?" "Have you ever had seizures back to back that would not stop?" "Are you taking folic acid?"

The patient pauses. Thinks. Some questions they answer easily. Others make them reach for a memory they had not thought to mention. The system notices the gaps - not to judge, but to gently press: "Could you tell us more about that part?"

What is happening here is not data collection. It is the first run. The patient is constructing their own narrative - discovering what they know, organizing what they have lived through. The scattered version comes out here, where there is no clock and no pressure. By the time they sit across from the physician, something has shifted. They are not narrating for the first time. They have already found the words. The physician still guides, still probes, still does the clinical work. But faster. Clearer. Because the patient arrived having already told the story once.

And if the visit window stays the same, the time saved is not absorbed by the system. It is returned. Returned to the patient. For their questions.

Because patients carry many questions into that room. Not just about the diagnosis, but about what it means. For their work. Their family. How to plan the next month, the next year. These need time. They need space. They need a physician who is not still extracting history with five minutes left.

We as doctors talk a lot. We do not listen much. And patients leave clinic the way they came in - with a handful of questions, unanswered.

What if fewer of them had to?
